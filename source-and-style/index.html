Fortune Cookie

Metadata

Object Type: Culinary Misattribution

Actual Origin: Japanese-American

Commonly Attributed Origin: Chinese-American

Time Period: 1900s-1910s

Location: Japanese tea gardens, San Francisco

Description

The fortune cookie is a fascinating example of culinary misattribution in American food history. Despite being ubiquitous in Chinese restaurants across the United States, the fortune cookie is actually of Japanese-American origin.

Created in Japanese tea gardens in San Francisco during the early 1900s-1910s, these cookies were inspired by Japanese crackers called "tsujiura senbei." The transformation occurred during and after World War II when Japanese Americans were relocated to internment camps and Chinese-American restaurant owners began producing these cookies. Over time, the fortune cookie became synonymous with Chinese restaurants in America.

Key Transformations

  • Cultural transfer from Japanese-American to Chinese-American identity during WWII
  • Now strongly associated with American Chinese restaurants despite being unknown in China
  • Changed from handmade in tea gardens to mass-produced in factories
  • Became an "invented tradition" representing Chinese culture to Americans
